* intro notes
- game theory is the mathematical modeling of strategic interaction among rational (and irrational) agents
- the von neumann–morgenstern (vnm) utility function (1947) is a mathematical tool used in economics and game theory to represent an individual's preferences over choices that involve risk or uncertainty. it demonstrates that rational agents make decisions by maximizing their expected utility rather than just expected monetary value

** introduction
*** tcp backoff game
- a game-theoretic model of network congestion control where multiple selfish nodes share a single communication channel
**** the setup
- goal: nodes want to transmit data as quickly as possible (minimize delay, maximize throughput)
- conflict: if two nodes transmit simultaneously, a collision occurs, and both transmissions fail
- mechanism: when a collision occurs, nodes must choose how long to wait (back off) before retransmitting
**** the strategies
- cooperative (standard tcp): nodes use exponential backoff (doubling their waiting time after each collision) to clear congestion
- defect/selfish (greedy): a node refuses to back off (or waits less) to grab the channel first
**** game-theoretic outcomes
- tragedy of the commons: if all nodes act selfishly, they constantly collide, leading to a throughput of near zero
- nash equilibrium: the cooperative standard tcp is not a nash equilibrium in a selfish environment, because an individual node always has an incentive to cheat (by backing off less) to get its packets through faster
- solution: network protocols use fair-queuing, punishment mechanisms, or mac-layer policing to enforce cooperation and prevent selfish behavior 
**** best possible strategy
- unpoliced / selfish network: defect always
- repeated (iterated): tit-for-tat / conditional cooperation
- modern policed network: cooperate (standard tcp)
*** self interested agents and utility theory
- agents have opinions and preferences 
- not that they want to harm others (adversarial) or only care about themselves
- only that the agent has its own description of states of the world that it likes, and acts based on this description
- utility function:
  - quantifies degree of preference across alternatives
  - explains impact of uncertainity
  - decision-theoretic rationality: act to maximize expected utility
*** key ingredients
- players: who? are decision makers (people, government, companies, ...) 
- actions: what? why? how? when? 
- payoffs: motivation (profits, care about others, ...)
- timing: chronological order of moves (nodes), actions (branches) and payoffs at the end of every path (terminal nodes) 
- information: imperfect or asymmetric info amongst players 
*** normal form representation
- a.k.a. matrix form, strategic form
- usually depicted as a multi-dimensional matrix (or a grid for two-player games), this representation assumes that players make their decisions simultaneously and independently, without knowing their opponents' choices beforehand
- by abstracting away the sequential timing of moves, the normal form provides a clear framework for identifying strategic dominance and calculating equilibria, such as the nash equilibrium
- finite n-person normal form game: <N,A,u>:
  - players: N={1,...n} is a finite set of n, indexed by i
  - action set for player i Ai
    - a = (a1,...an) ∈ A = A1 x .... x An is an action profile    
  - utility function or payoff function for player i:ui:A->ℝ
    - u = (u1,...un) is a profile of utility functions     
*** extensive form representation
- the extensive form representation models a game as a tree structure to capture sequential decision-making and the flow of time
- timing: it explicitly details the players, the chronological order of their moves (represented by decision nodes), the actions available at each turn (represented by branches), and the final payoffs at the end of each path (represented by terminal nodes)
- information: crucially, unlike the normal form, the extensive form can represent imperfect or asymmetric information using "information sets," which group nodes together to show when a player must make a choice without knowing the exact previous moves of their opponents

** summary of sample games (gemini assisted writeup)
*** tcp backoff
- a congestion control mechanism where the retransmission timeout (rto) doubles after each consecutive failed attempt to send a packet
- purpose: prevents network collapse by reducing the frequency of retransmission requests when the network is congested
- mechanism: when a packet is lost, the sender waits for the rto. if the retry fails, the rto is doubled (exponentially) before the next attempt
- reset: once a packet is successfully acknowledged, the rto typically resets to its base value
- context: it is a core component of the exponential backoff algorithm used in tcp to maintain stability under high traffic loads
*** matching pennies game
- a classic zero-sum game in game theory:
- setup: two players (a and b) each place a penny on a table simultaneously
- rules:
  - player a (even): wins if the pennies match (both heads or both tails)
  - player b (odd): wins if the pennies do not match (one head, one tail)
- strategy: there is no pure strategy equilibrium. if one player chooses a fixed strategy, the other can exploit it
- nash equilibrium: both players should randomize their choices (heads/tails) with a probability of 0.5 to ensure the opponent cannot gain an advantage
*** coordination game
- a type of game theory model where players earn higher payoffs by choosing the same strategy as others
- multiple equilibria: there are at least two "best" outcomes where no player has an incentive to deviate (e.g., meeting at location a or location b)
- mutual benefit: the goal is to align choices, not to compete for a limited resource
- common interest: players prefer to coordinate rather than mismatch, even if they have different preferences regarding /which/ equilibrium is chosen
- examples: stag hunt, battle of the sexes, driving on the road, technology standard (usb type c), language, social norms (dressing)
*** battle of the sexes
-  a coordination game where two players prefer different outcomes but both prefer coordinating over failing to do so
-  scenario: a couple wants to meet for the evening. player a prefers the opera; player b prefers a football match. both prefer being together at /either/ event over being apart
-  the payoff matrix: there are two *pure strategy nash equilibria*:
   - both go to the opera (a is happiest)
   - both go to the football match (b is happiest)
-  the dilemma: neither player knows which event the other will choose. this leads to a coordination problem where players must rely on "focal points" or communication to avoid mismatching
-  mixed strategy: there is also a mixed strategy nash equilibrium where players randomize their choices to account for the uncertainty, though this often results in suboptimal coordination
*** keynesian beauty contest
- a game theory thought experiment illustrating how markets and asset prices are driven by *strategic interaction* rather than objective value
- rules: participants must guess a number from 0 to 100. the winner is the person whose guess is closest to *2/3 of the average* of all guesses
- logic:
  - level 0: a random guess (average 50)
  - level 1: if everyone guesses randomly, the average is 50; 2/3 of that is 33.3
  - level 2: if everyone thinks rationally (at level 1), the average will be 33.3; 2/3 of that is 22.2
  - nash equilibrium: this recursion continues until it reaches 0
- key takeaway: the "rational" move isn't just about what you think; it’s about what you think others think (higher-order beliefs). in financial markets, this explains speculative bubbles: participants buy assets not because of their intrinsic value, but because they believe others will drive the price higher
*** penalty kick
- a classic zero-sum game modeled as a mixed-strategy nash equilibrium
- minimax theorem: to avoid being predictable, both the kicker and the goalkeeper must randomize their strategies (e.g., kicker: left vs. right; goalkeeper: left vs. right)
- indifference principle: to keep the opponent guessing, you must choose your strategies such that the opponent is indifferent between their own options. if the kicker's success rate is higher when aiming left, they should aim left until the goalkeeper adjusts
- the equilibrium: the optimal strategy is to randomize choices such that the expected payoff is equal regardless of the opponent's move
  - if a player becomes predictable, the opponent will exploit it
  - "matching pennies": the simplest formal model. if the kicker kicks to a side, and the keeper dives the same way, the keeper wins. if they dive the wrong way, the kicker wins
*** traveller's dilemma
- a game theory scenario that reveals a paradox in rational thinking
- setup: two travelers, lucy and pete, have identical antiques broken by an airline. the airline manager, unable to assess the value, asks them to independently write down its worth, from $2 to $100
- rules:
  - if they write the same number, that is the true value, and they both get that amount
  - if they write different numbers, the lower number is taken as the true value. both will receive that lower amount, but...
    - the person who wrote the lower number gets a $2 bonus
    - the person who wrote the higher number gets a $2 penalty
- the dilemma:
  - intuitive thought: both should write $100 to maximize their joint payout. they would each get $100
  - rational thought: if pete writes $100, lucy can write $99. she would get $99 + $2 bonus = $101, while pete gets $99 - $2 penalty = $97
  - anticipating this, pete might think to write $99. but then lucy should write $98 to get $98 + $2 = $100
  - this logic continues, creating a "race to the bottom"
- the nash equilibrium: the only stable, purely rational outcome is for both players to write $2. at this point, neither player can improve their outcome by changing their answer. this leads to a payoff far worse than the cooperative one
- the dilemma highlights the conflict between individual rational self-interest and a better, cooperative outcome
*** centipede game theory
- a sequential game in game theory that illustrates a conflict between rational self-interest and actual human behavior.
- setup
  - two players take turns choosing from a steadily increasing pot of money
  - on each turn, a player can either "take" the larger share of the current pot (ending the game) or "pass" to the other player, causing the total pot to grow
- paradox
  - rational outcome (backward induction): the purely rational strategy is for the first player to "take" the money on the very first move. the logic is that the last player will always take the money, so the second-to-last player should take it one step before, and this reasoning cascades all the way to the start
  - empirical result: in experiments, players almost never do this. they tend to "pass" for several rounds, cooperating to grow the pot before one player eventually defects and "takes" it
- the game demonstrates that pure, self-interested rationality is a poor predictor of human behavior, which often involves elements of trust, altruism, and hope for mutual cooperation
*** ultimatum game
- a game theory experiment where two players split a sum of money:
  - the proposer: offers a split of the money to the responder
  - the responder: can accept the offer (both get the split) or reject it (both get nothing)
- key takeaways:
  - rational choice: standard economic theory suggests the responder should accept any non-zero offer, and the proposer should offer the smallest possible amount
  - actual behavior: in practice, humans usually reject "unfair" offers (typically below 20–30%), preferring to receive nothing rather than accept an insulting split
  - psychology: this demonstrates that human decision-making is driven by fairness, social norms, and altruistic punishment rather than purely selfish utility maximization
*** rainbow warship
- a variation of signaling games in game theory, often used to illustrate cooperation, trust, and deterrence under asymmetric information
- the setup:
  - two ships (often colored) encounter each other in fog or at distance
  - the dilemma: each ship has a "fire" or "don’t fire" option
  - information gap: each ship knows its own intentions but cannot be 100% certain of the other's color or true intent
  - signaling: ships can deploy "rainbow" signals (or lights) to identify themselves
- key game theory concepts
  - signaling games: the "rainbow" acts as a signal. if the signal is costly (e.g., revealing your position makes you vulnerable), it is a "credible signal" (or spence signaling). if it is "cheap talk," it is easily faked by an aggressor
  - prisoner’s dilemma: if both fire, both lose. if one fires and the other doesn't, the aggressor gains a temporary advantage. the most rational cooperative outcome is mutual non-aggression, but the fear of betrayal creates a "security dilemma."
  - bayesian equilibrium: players update their beliefs about the other ship's identity (or intent) based on the signal provided. if the probability of an enemy being "hostile" is high, the incentive to strike first increases
  - tit-for-tat: a common strategy in this game. you start by signaling peace; if the other side reciprocates, you continue; if they strike, you retaliate
- takeaway: the game demonstrates that trust is a function of signal reliability. in environments where communication is ambiguous, players tend to choose "defensive aggression" (striking first) to avoid being the sucker, leading to sub-optimal outcomes for both
*** rock paper scissors
- a zero-sum game with a mixed-strategy nash equilibrium
- the strategy: to be unexploitable, a player must choose each option with equal probability (1/3). any deviation (e.g., playing rock more often) allows an opponent to exploit the bias by playing paper more frequently
- human psychology: humans are poor random-number generators. studies (such as those from zhejiang university) show that winners tend to repeat their winning move, while losers tend to switch to the move that would have beaten what just beat them ("win-stay, lose-shift")
- the optimal counter: if you recognize a pattern in an opponent’s bias, the nash equilibrium breaks, and you can achieve a higher win rate by exploiting their tendency
*** multiple-round auctions
- extend single-round models by introducing intertemporal strategic considerations
- key dynamics include:
  - information revelation: bidders learn about competitors' valuations through their bidding patterns, leading to the "winner's curse" or strategic signaling
  - dynamic learning: participants update their beliefs about others' types (bayesian learning) as the auction progresses
  - signaling and predation: bidders may bid aggressively early on to discourage entry or signal strength (a "fat-cat" or "puppy-dog" effect)
- common auction types:
  - english auction: ascending price; naturally multi-round; truthful bidding is often dominant
  - dutch auction: descending price; equivalent to first-price sealed-bid under symmetric conditions
  - simultaneous multi-round auction (smra): used by the fcc for spectrum; allows bidders to switch between items, revealing price discovery across a market
  - tacit collusion: multi-round structures facilitate "bid signaling," where participants communicate preferences to avoid bidding against one another (e.g., using specific digits in bids)
- key challenge: the complexity of the "bidder’s curse" and the difficulty of calculating equilibrium strategies in real-time, often requiring numerical methods or simplified heuristic models
